Chapter 1 — The Signal

A world of blue — but not all equal

This is the Copernicus Marine Service live viewer, showing the concentration of dissolved molecular oxygen at the ocean surface in November 2022 — the exact dataset used for this story.

The bright equatorial band is the upwelling zone — cold, oxygen-rich deep water rising to the surface, driving some of the planet's most productive fisheries.

The variable o2 in the NetCDF file ranges from 0.28 to 552 mmol/m³ across 15 depth levels, on a 0.25° grid spanning 557 latitudes × 881 longitudes.

The darker zones flanking the equator are where the ocean begins to lose its breath. You can zoom, pan, and explore the data directly in the viewer on the left.

Chapter 2 — The Dead Zones

Three regions where the ocean can barely breathe

Oxygen Minimum Zones (OMZs) are semi-permanent regions where O₂ falls below 63 mmol/m³ — the critical threshold below which most fish, crustaceans and invertebrates cannot survive.

Three major OMZs dominate the global ocean. Each is driven by a distinct combination of ocean circulation, temperature, and biological decomposition.

The Eastern Tropical Pacific OMZ alone spans an area larger than the United States. It has quadrupled in size since 1960.

Hover over each highlighted zone on the map to see its current O₂ level and the ecosystem it threatens.

Chapter 3 — Into the Deep

Oxygen hidden in the water column

The Copernicus NetCDF file contains 15 depth levels, from 0 to 2,000 m. This vertical structure reveals what surface maps cannot show: in OMZ regions, the oxygen collapses almost to zero in a layer hundreds of meters thick.

In normal open-ocean regions, O₂ is replenished at the surface by atmosphere exchange and photosynthesis. It drops through the mesopelagic zone, then partially recovers in cold abyssal water below.

In OMZ cores, this recovery never happens. The dead zone sits permanently between 200 and 800 m — cutting the ocean's livable volume in half.

Select a region using the buttons on the chart to compare vertical profiles across the world ocean.

Chapter 4 — The Trend

Sixty years of slow asphyxiation

Since systematic ocean measurements began, the global ocean has lost an estimated −2% of its dissolved oxygen. In the OMZ cores, the drop is far steeper.

Warmer water holds less oxygen. As climate change heats the ocean surface, thermal stratification intensifies — preventing the mixing that renews deep-layer oxygen from above.

The Arabian Sea OMZ is so depleted — sometimes below 5 mmol/m³ — that its bacteria now produce nitrous oxide (N₂O), a greenhouse gas 300× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years.

The Copernicus reanalysis product covers 2000–2022, giving researchers a 22-year window to track these changes with unprecedented spatial resolution.

Chapter 5 — The Squeeze

Marine life compressed into a thinner and thinner layer

As OMZs expand upward, fish and other organisms are forced into an ever-thinner surface layer — creating dangerous crowding, food competition, and exposure to warming and fishing pressure.

This vertical compression is one of the least visible consequences of deoxygenation. It doesn't make headlines, but it restructures entire food webs.

The habitable column for many commercial fish species has shrunk by 20–40% in the Eastern Pacific over five decades. The Copernicus 15-level data makes it possible to quantify this squeeze year by year.
Chapter 6 — The Evidence

The same data — a different depth

Now the same Copernicus viewer, but at ~200 m depth — the core of the oxygen minimum zone. Compare this with what you saw at the surface in Chapter 1.

The vast dark regions that appeared faint at the surface now dominate the map. The Eastern Tropical Pacific, the Arabian Sea, and the Bay of Bengal are near-black: zones of extreme oxygen depletion.

This is what the NetCDF file's 15 depth levels unlock: a three-dimensional portrait of where the ocean can — and cannot — support life.

You can use the depth slider inside the viewer to move through the water column yourself and watch the OMZs emerge.

What the data tells us

The ocean's distress signal is already in the data

The Copernicus Marine BGC reanalysis product gives scientists and storytellers an unparalleled window into the ocean's changing chemistry. Every millimole of oxygen lost is a measurable, mappable, verifiable fact.

This story was built from a single NetCDF file — 15 depth levels, 557 × 881 grid cells, one day in November 2022. The full reanalysis spans decades. The signal it contains is unambiguous.
